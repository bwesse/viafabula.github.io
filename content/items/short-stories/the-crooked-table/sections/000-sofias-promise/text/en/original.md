Sofia repaired old furniture in a small workshop behind her apartment. Every morning, she opened the green door at seven, made coffee, and placed her tools in a straight line. From that moment, the story moved in a new direction.

She liked work that was exact. A chair should not shake. A drawer should close quietly. If a line was almost straight, Sofia saw only the part that was not.

In June, the neighbors planned a dinner in the courtyard. Everyone promised to bring something. Malik would cook soup, Eva would bring bread, and the children would make paper lanterns.

“We need a long table,” Malik said. “We can put three small tables together.” Sofia looked at their different heights and colors. “No,” she answered. “I will build one.”

The dinner was in two weeks. Sofia found strong wood and drew a careful plan. She imagined twenty people sitting around a smooth, beautiful table. Perhaps the neighbors would finally understand why good work needed time.

After that, Sofia stayed in the workshop every evening. When Malik asked her to drink tea, she said, “Tomorrow.” When the children showed her their lanterns, she said, “Later. There is too much dust here.”

The table grew longer and smoother. The days grew shorter. Sofia did not notice either change.
