The battery sat on a low handcart. It was the size of a suitcase and much heavier. Inés pushed from behind while Lea and Omar pulled from the front with a rope. Already, the situation was becoming more complicated than it first appeared.

They went straight through the fish market. The shops were closed, but the stones still smelled of salt and oranges. At the blue stairs, the cart stopped. The steps were too narrow.

“There is another road,” said Inés. “Turn right after the bakery, pass the square, and follow the wall above the roofs. It is longer, but there are no stairs.”

They changed direction. The wind became stronger as they climbed. Omar pulled the rope, Lea kept the cart away from the wall, and Inés watched the radio.

At the square, two streets looked exactly the same in the fog. Lea wanted to take the street on the left. Omar pointed to a painted fish on the right-hand wall. “The map says we follow the fish,” he said.

The right-hand street led above the village. Below them, warm windows shone through the fog. Far out at sea, a small radio light moved on Inés's screen.

“That is the Mar Azul,” she said. “My brother is the captain. They will reach the bay in less than an hour.” Nobody mentioned the closed kitchen again.
